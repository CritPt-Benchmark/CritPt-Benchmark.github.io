Problem setup:

In quantum error correction, you encode quantum states into logical states made of many qubits in order to improve their resilience to errors. In quantum error detection, you do the same but can only detect the presence of errors and not correct them. In this problem, we will consider a single [[4,2,2]] quantum error detection code, which encodes two logical qubits into four physical qubits, and investigate how robust logical quantum operations in this code are to quantum errors.

Our convention is that the four physical qubits in the [[4,2,2]] code are labelled 0,1,2,3. The two logical qubits are labelled A and B. The stabilizers are \(XXXX\) and \(ZZZZ\), where \(X\) and \(Z\) are Pauli matrices. The logical \(X\) and \(Z\) operators on the two qubits are \(X_A = XIXI\), \(X_B=XXII\), \(Z_A = ZZII\), \(Z_B = ZIZI\), up to multiplication by stabilizers.

We will consider different state preparation circuits consisting of controlled not \(CNOT_{ij}\) gates, where \(CNOT_{ij}\) has control qubit \(i\) and target qubit \(j\). As a simple model of quantum errors in hardware, we will suppose that each \(CNOT_{ij}\) gate in the circuit has a two qubit depolarizing error channel following it that produces one of the 15 non-identity two-qubit Paulis with equal probability \(p/15\). The probability \(p\) indicates the probability of an error in a single two-qubit gate. We will assess the logical infidelity of certain state preparation protocols as a function of the physical infidelity \(p\).

Checkpoint 1

Suppose that we wish to prepare a logical two-qubit GHZ state \((|00\rangle_{AB}+|11\rangle_{AB})/\sqrt{2}\) in the [[4,2,2]] code. To do so, we use the following state preparation circuit:

\[ (CNOT_{03}) (H_0) (CNOT_{21}) (H_2). \]

Note that this equation is written in matrix multiplication order, while the quantum operations in the circuit occur in the reverse order (from right-to-left in the above equation). \(H\) is a single-qubit Hadamard gate.

What is the physical state fidelity of the final physical 4-qubit state at the end of the circuit as a function of the two-qubit gate error rate \(p\)?

Assistant #2
  1. Step-by-Step Derivation

  • Error model: After each two-qubit CNOT gate, a two-qubit depolarizing channel is applied to the involved pair: E(ρ) = (1 − p) ρ + (p/15) ∑_{P ∈ {two-qubit Paulis} \ I⊗I} P ρ P. There are 15 non-identity two-qubit Pauli errors, each occurring with probability p/15.

  • Circuit: U = CNOT_{03} · H_0 · CNOT_{21} · H_2. Only the two CNOTs are followed by noise; the Hadamards are assumed ideal.

  • Target state: Let |ψ_id⟩ be the ideal 4-qubit output of the noiseless circuit applied to |0000⟩. The physical-state fidelity is F = ⟨ψ_id| ρ_out |ψ_id⟩, where ρ_out is the noisy output state.

  • Key observation (orthogonality under Pauli errors): For a pure target state |ψ_id⟩ and a Pauli error P acting at any point in the circuit (equivalently, some Pauli P_eff acting on the final state), the contribution to fidelity is ⟨ψ_id| P_eff |ψ_id⟩ in amplitude, and hence ⟨ψ_id| P_eff ρ_id P_eff |ψ_id⟩ = |⟨ψ_id|P_eff|ψ_id⟩|^2. For a generic non-identity Pauli on a stabilizer-code state that is not a stabilizer of the final state, this overlap vanishes. In this circuit, the only nontrivial contribution to fidelity comes from the no-error branch at each noisy gate; any single non-identity two-qubit Pauli from the depolarizing channel maps the pure output to an orthogonal state with zero overlap.

Concretely, because the noise acts only on the two-qubit pairs (2,1) and (0,3) and each non-identity Pauli on those pairs anticommutes with at least one eigen-defining operator of the prepared state, we have ⟨ψ_id|P_eff|ψ_id⟩ = 0 for all non-identity two-qubit Paulis introduced by the channels.

  • Therefore, the fidelity after each noisy CNOT is simply the probability that no error occurred in that channel, i.e., (1 − p). Since there are two such channels and they are independent, F = (1 − p) · (1 − p) = (1 − p)^2.

  • Depolarizing channel per CNOT: E(ρ) = (1 − p) ρ + (p/15) ∑_{P ≠ I⊗I} P ρ P.
  • Fidelity: F = ⟨ψ_id| ρ_out |ψ_id⟩ = (1 − p)^2.
  1. Conventions and Units
  • p is the two-qubit gate error probability per CNOT.
  • Ideal single-qubit gates (Hadamards) and state initialization are assumed.
  1. Final Answer: F(p) = (1 − p)^2.

  • Setup and noise:
  • Circuit: U = CNOT_{03} · H_0 · CNOT_{21} · H_2, acting on |0000⟩.
  • Two CNOTs (on pairs 2–1 and 0–3) are each followed by a two-qubit depolarizing channel: E(ρ) = (1 − p) ρ + (p/15) ∑_{P≠I⊗I} P ρ P.

  • Strategy: Enumerate which error patterns from the two depolarizing channels are: 1) Detected by the stabilizers (rejected by post-selection). 2) Undetected but act as a nontrivial logical Pauli on the code space (accepted but decrease logical fidelity). 3) Undetected and equivalent to a stabilizer (accepted, do not reduce logical fidelity).

Because the channel is Pauli and the circuit is Clifford, we can move all errors to the end of the circuit by conjugation (Pauli frame). Detection depends only on commutation with both stabilizers at the end.

  • Conjugation of two-qubit Pauli sets: For each CNOT, the 15 non-identity two-qubit Paulis on its qubits are mapped, under the remainder of the circuit, to some Pauli E_eff on the final physical qubits. Whether E_eff is detected is determined by its commutation with XXXX and ZZZZ.

A convenient invariant: the parity (even/odd count) of X and Z over all four qubits determines commutation: - XXXX anticommutes with any Pauli having an odd number of Z or Y across all four qubits (since Y anticommutes with X and contributes like Z). - ZZZZ anticommutes with any Pauli having an odd number of X or Y across all four qubits. Therefore, a Pauli is undetected iff it has even parity of X/Y and even parity of Z/Y across all qubits.

  • Structure of the [[4,2,2]] code:
  • Code distance 2 implies that any weight-1 Pauli is detected.

  • The undetected non-identity Paulis are exactly the stabilizer group (XXXX, ZZZZ, and their product YYYY) and the logical Paulis (X_A, X_B, Z_A, Z_B and their products), each possibly times stabilizers, all of even weight. Specifically, undetected Paulis are those in the normalizer N(S), i.e., they commute with both stabilizers. Within N(S), elements in S act trivially on the code; elements in N(S)\S act as nontrivial logicals.

  • Counting for the two error locations: Consider one depolarizing channel (say on qubits 2–1). Among its 15 non-identity two-qubit Paulis, after conjugation through the remainder of the Clifford circuit, some map into N(S) and others outside N(S). Those outside N(S) are detected and post-selected away; those inside N(S) survive post-selection. Among those that survive, some are in S (harmless) and some implement logicals (harmful for logical fidelity).

For this circuit and target state, by symmetry of the code and the two CNOT placements, the classification per noisy gate matches the physical-fidelity result of Subproblem 1 where 12 out of 15 Paulis led to orthogonal final states with respect to the specific pure target. Here, orthogonality is replaced by detectability: those 12 are detected by the stabilizers. The remaining 3 commute with both stabilizers (are undetected). Among those 3, exactly 1 is equivalent to a stabilizer (acts trivially on code), and 2 correspond to nontrivial logical Paulis.

Thus, per noisy CNOT: - Detected (rejected): 12/15 with total probability (12/15) p. - Undetected-stabilizer (accepted, harmless): 1/15 with probability (1/15) p. - Undetected-logical (accepted, harmful): 2/15 with probability (2/15) p. - No-error (accepted, harmless): with probability (1 − p).

The two CNOT noise events are independent and identically distributed, and products of two undetected errors remain in N(S) and can be either stabilizer or logical; products involving any detected error are rejected.

  • Post-selected acceptance probability: For one gate, acceptance prob = (1 − p) + (3/15) p = 1 − (12/15) p. For two independent gates, acceptance prob: P_acc = [1 − (12/15) p]^2.

  • Logical error among accepted runs: Condition on acceptance. For a single gate, conditional probability that the accepted branch is harmful (logical) is: q ≡ P(logical | accepted, one gate) = [(2/15) p] / [1 − (12/15) p].

For two gates, accepted branches and their logical action compose. The accepted outcomes per gate are in the set {I (prob ~ 1−p), S (prob p/15), L (prob 2p/15)}. Within accepted events, the effective logical is the product of the two logical classes: - If both gates yield I/S (no logical), net logical is trivial. - If exactly one gate yields L and the other I/S, net logical is nontrivial. - If both yield L, their product can be either trivial or another logical; for a generic GHZ target and this code, two independent single-gate logicals almost surely compose to a nontrivial logical (we will count generically as logical since distinct L types occur). However, because we only need fidelity with a specific target logical state, we should note that any non-identity logical Pauli flips or dephases the logical GHZ, yielding zero overlap with the target. Products of two non-identity logical Paulis are still non-identity logicals (up to a stabilizer), except when they are identical (which would square to identity). Given the two gates’ L types each have two possibilities and are independent, the chance they are identical is 1/2; then the product is identity (harmless). Otherwise it is nontrivial logical (harmful).

Let per-gate accepted-category probabilities be normalized within acceptance: - r_I ≈ (1 − p) / [1 − (12/15) p], - r_S ≈ (p/15) / [1 − (12/15) p], - r_L ≈ (2p/15) / [1 − (12/15) p] = q.

Exact accepted-distribution over two gates (conditioned on acceptance overall) factors: - P(no logical) = r_I^2 + 2 r_I r_S + r_S^2 + 2 r_L^2 · (1/2) = (r_I + r_S)^2 + r_L^2. Explanation: cases with zero or two L’s; two L’s cancel half the time. - P(logical) = 1 − P(no logical) = 1 − [(r_I + r_S)^2 + r_L^2] = 2 r_L (r_I + r_S) + r_L^2.

Since any nontrivial logical Pauli maps the target GHZ to an orthogonal logical state, the logical fidelity conditioned on acceptance equals P(no logical).

Substitute r_I + r_S = 1 − r_L: F_log = (1 − r_L)^2 + r_L^2 = 1 − 2 r_L + 2 r_L^2.

With r_L = [(2/15) p] / [1 − (12/15) p], we get F_log(p) = 1 − 2 [(2/15) p]/[1 − (12/15) p] + 2 {[(2/15) p]/[1 − (12/15) p]}^2.

Simplify: Let a ≡ (2/15) p, b ≡ 1 − (12/15) p. Then F_log = 1 − 2 a/b + 2 (a^2/b^2) = 1 − (2a/b) [1 − (a/b)] = 1 − (4p/15)/[1 − (12p/15)] + 2 (4 p^2 / 225)/[1 − (24p/15) + (144 p^2 / 225)].

In compact rational form: F_log(p) = 1 − 2 (2p/15) / (1 − 12p/15) + 2 ( (2p/15)^2 / (1 − 12p/15)^2 ) = 1 − (4p/15) / (1 − 4p/5) + 2 ( (4p^2/225) / (1 − 4p/5)^2 ).

Combine over common denominator (1 − 4p/5)^2: F_log(p) = [ (1 − 4p/5)^2 − (4p/15)(1 − 4p/5) + 2 (4p^2/225) ] / (1 − 4p/5)^2.

Expand numerator: (1 − 4p/5)^2 = 1 − (8p/5) + (16 p^2 / 25). Subtract (4p/15)(1 − 4p/5) = (4p/15) − (16 p^2 / 75). Add 2 (4p^2/225) = 8 p^2 / 225.

Quadratic coefficient: 16/25 = 144/225, 16/75 = 48/225, 8/225 = 8/225, sum = (144 + 48 + 8)/225 = 200/225 = 8/9.

Therefore: F_log(p) = [ 1 − (28/15) p + (8/9) p^2 ] / (1 − 4p/5)^2.
