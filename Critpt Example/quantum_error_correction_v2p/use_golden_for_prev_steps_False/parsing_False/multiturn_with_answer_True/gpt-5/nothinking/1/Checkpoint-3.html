Problem setup:

In quantum error correction, you encode quantum states into logical states made of many qubits in order to improve their resilience to errors. In quantum error detection, you do the same but can only detect the presence of errors and not correct them. In this problem, we will consider a single [[4,2,2]] quantum error detection code, which encodes two logical qubits into four physical qubits, and investigate how robust logical quantum operations in this code are to quantum errors.

Our convention is that the four physical qubits in the [[4,2,2]] code are labelled 0,1,2,3. The two logical qubits are labelled A and B. The stabilizers are \(XXXX\) and \(ZZZZ\), where \(X\) and \(Z\) are Pauli matrices. The logical \(X\) and \(Z\) operators on the two qubits are \(X_A = XIXI\), \(X_B=XXII\), \(Z_A = ZZII\), \(Z_B = ZIZI\), up to multiplication by stabilizers.

We will consider different state preparation circuits consisting of controlled not \(CNOT_{ij}\) gates, where \(CNOT_{ij}\) has control qubit \(i\) and target qubit \(j\). As a simple model of quantum errors in hardware, we will suppose that each \(CNOT_{ij}\) gate in the circuit has a two qubit depolarizing error channel following it that produces one of the 15 non-identity two-qubit Paulis with equal probability \(p/15\). The probability \(p\) indicates the probability of an error in a single two-qubit gate. We will assess the logical infidelity of certain state preparation protocols as a function of the physical infidelity \(p\).

Checkpoint 1

Suppose that we wish to prepare a logical two-qubit GHZ state \((|00\rangle_{AB}+|11\rangle_{AB})/\sqrt{2}\) in the [[4,2,2]] code. To do so, we use the following state preparation circuit:

\[ (CNOT_{03}) (H_0) (CNOT_{21}) (H_2). \]

Note that this equation is written in matrix multiplication order, while the quantum operations in the circuit occur in the reverse order (from right-to-left in the above equation). \(H\) is a single-qubit Hadamard gate.

What is the physical state fidelity of the final physical 4-qubit state at the end of the circuit as a function of the two-qubit gate error rate \(p\)?

  1. Step-by-Step Derivation
  • Code and target logical state:
  • The [[4,2,2]] code encodes two logical qubits A,B into 4 physical qubits (labeled 0,1,2,3) with stabilizers S = ⟨XXXX, ZZZZ⟩.
  • We want to prepare the logical GHZ state (|00⟩AB + |11⟩AB)/√2 in the code space.

  • The given circuit acts on the 4 physical qubits as: U = CNOT03 · H0 · CNOT21 · H2, applied right-to-left.

  • Error model:

  • After each two-qubit CNOT gate, a two-qubit depolarizing channel is applied to the same two qubits: E(ρ) = (1 − p) ρ + (p/15) ∑P≠II P ρ P, summing over the 15 non-identity two-qubit Paulis on the two involved qubits.
  • Single-qubit H gates are ideal (no error).

  • The two noisy CNOTs are CNOT21 and CNOT03.

  • Physical state fidelity definition:

  • Let |ψid⟩ be the ideal 4-qubit output state obtained with perfect gates.
  • Let ρ be the actual output density matrix including the two depolarizing channels after the two CNOTs.

  • The physical state fidelity is F = ⟨ψid| ρ |ψid⟩.

  • Commutation and invariance considerations:

  • A unitary U acting after a Pauli channel preserves the average fidelity with respect to the corresponding ideal output state, because fidelity with a pure state depends only on the error channel “in the Heisenberg picture” conjugated by the subsequent unitaries.
  • Therefore, to compute F, it suffices to evaluate the overlap of |ψid⟩ with states produced by Pauli errors inserted after each CNOT, propagated through the remaining gates. For a pure target |ψid⟩, a Pauli error P either:
    • maps |ψid⟩ to an orthogonal state (giving zero overlap), or
    • stabilizes the state up to a phase (giving unit overlap).

  • Hence, for each error location, the fidelity contribution equals the probability that the effective Pauli after propagation is the identity on the final state (i.e., acts trivially on |ψid⟩).

  • Key observation for this circuit:

  • The target ideal state |ψid⟩ is a specific code state stabilized by XXXX and ZZZZ and is a joint +1 eigenstate of these stabilizers.
  • A two-qubit depolarizing error produces one of the 15 non-identity two-qubit Paulis on the involved pair. After propagation by the remainder of the circuit, the error becomes some 4-qubit Pauli on all qubits (via CNOT conjugation rules). For the overlap with |ψid⟩ to remain 1, this effective Pauli must be in the stabilizer group of |ψid⟩ (or equal to identity). Otherwise it anticommutes with at least one stabilizer/logical operator and yields an orthogonal state, giving zero overlap.
  • For this specific preparation in the [[4,2,2]] code, any nontrivial two-qubit Pauli inserted at either of the two CNOT locations propagates to an operator that is not in the stabilizer group {±I, ±XXXX, ±ZZZZ, ±(XXXX)(ZZZZ)}. In particular, no nontrivial two-qubit Pauli on qubits (2,1) or (0,3) can equal I, XXXX, ZZZZ, or XXXX·ZZZZ up to phases when expressed on all four qubits after propagation through the remaining gates. Therefore, all non-identity errors are detected by reducing the fidelity to zero overlap with |ψid⟩.

Justification sketch: - Conjugation by H only swaps X↔Z on the acted qubit; conjugation by CNOT maps single/two-qubit Paulis to Paulis with support that generally increases but cannot reduce a weight-2 Pauli to a stabilizer of weight 0 or 4 in this circuit structure. - The stabilizers have weight 4; achieving them from a weight-2 error would require the circuit to deterministically spread the error to exactly XXXX or ZZZZ on the four qubits, which does not occur for any of the 15 choices at either location for this gate sequence.

  • Independence of the two noisy locations:
  • The two depolarizing channels are independent and applied once each, after CNOT21 and after CNOT03.
  • Let F1 be the fidelity factor from the first channel and F2 from the second. Because an error at either location (if non-identity) yields zero overlap with |ψid⟩, the only way to retain overlap 1 is to have no error at that location. Therefore:
    • F1 = (1 − p)
    • F2 = (1 − p)

  • Any sequence with one or both errors non-identity yields zero overlap with |ψid⟩ in this circuit.

  • Overall physical state fidelity:

  • The channels act sequentially, and only the event “no error at both locations” preserves the target state.
  • Therefore: F(p) = (1 − p) × (1 − p) = (1 − p)^2.
  1. Mathematical Typesetting

Let |ψid⟩ be the ideal 4-qubit output. With depolarizing noise after the two CNOTs, the final state is ρ = E03 ∘ Upost · [E21 ∘ Upre (|0000⟩⟨0000|)] · (⋯) where E21 and E03 are the two-qubit depolarizing channels after CNOT21 and CNOT03, respectively, and Upre, Upost are the surrounding ideal unitaries.

The physical state fidelity is F(p) = ⟨ψid| ρ |ψid⟩.

Since any non-identity error at either noisy location maps |ψid⟩ to an orthogonal state, only identity-at-both contributes:

\[ F(p) = \Pr[\text{no error after CNOT}_{21}] \cdot \Pr[\text{no error after CNOT}_{03}] = (1 - p)^2. \]
  1. Conventions and Units
  • Error rate p is the two-qubit gate error probability for the depolarizing channel as specified.
  1. Final Answer: F(p) = (1 − p)^2.
