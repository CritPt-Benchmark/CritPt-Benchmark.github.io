Problem setup:

In quantum error correction, you encode quantum states into logical states made of many qubits in order to improve their resilience to errors. In quantum error detection, you do the same but can only detect the presence of errors and not correct them. In this problem, we will consider a single [[4,2,2]] quantum error detection code, which encodes two logical qubits into four physical qubits, and investigate how robust logical quantum operations in this code are to quantum errors.

Our convention is that the four physical qubits in the [[4,2,2]] code are labelled 0,1,2,3. The two logical qubits are labelled A and B. The stabilizers are \(XXXX\) and \(ZZZZ\), where \(X\) and \(Z\) are Pauli matrices. The logical \(X\) and \(Z\) operators on the two qubits are \(X_A = XIXI\), \(X_B=XXII\), \(Z_A = ZZII\), \(Z_B = ZIZI\), up to multiplication by stabilizers.

We will consider different state preparation circuits consisting of controlled not \(CNOT_{ij}\) gates, where \(CNOT_{ij}\) has control qubit \(i\) and target qubit \(j\). As a simple model of quantum errors in hardware, we will suppose that each \(CNOT_{ij}\) gate in the circuit has a two qubit depolarizing error channel following it that produces one of the 15 non-identity two-qubit Paulis with equal probability \(p/15\). The probability \(p\) indicates the probability of an error in a single two-qubit gate. We will assess the logical infidelity of certain state preparation protocols as a function of the physical infidelity \(p\).

Checkpoints

Checkpoint 1

Suppose that we wish to prepare a logical two-qubit GHZ state \((|00\rangle_{AB}+|11\rangle_{AB})/\sqrt{2}\) in the [[4,2,2]] code. To do so, we use the following state preparation circuit:

\[ (CNOT_{03}) (H_0) (CNOT_{21}) (H_2). \]

Note that this equation is written in matrix multiplication order, while the quantum operations in the circuit occur in the reverse order (from right-to-left in the above equation). \(H\) is a single-qubit Hadamard gate.

What is the physical state fidelity of the final physical 4-qubit state at the end of the circuit as a function of the two-qubit gate error rate \(p\)?

  1. Step-by-Step Derivation
  • Code and target state:
  • We work with the [[4,2,2]] code with stabilizers XXXX and ZZZZ on qubits 0,1,2,3.
  • We want to prepare the logical GHZ state (|00⟩AB + |11⟩AB)/√2 using the circuit
\[ U = CNOT_{03}\, H_0\, CNOT_{21}\, H_2, \]
applied right-to-left on an initial product state. (The specific initial state is irrelevant for the fidelity calculation below because we compare the noisy output to the ideal output of the same circuit.)
  • Error model:
  • After each two-qubit gate, a two-qubit depolarizing channel acts: with probability 1−p: no error; with probability p: one of the 15 non-identity two-qubit Pauli errors, each with probability p/15.

  • The single-qubit Hadamards are assumed ideal (no errors).

  • Locations of noise:

  • In the circuit, there are exactly two two-qubit gates: CNOT21 and CNOT03.

  • Therefore, there are two independent error locations, each with the above depolarizing noise.

  • Physical state fidelity definition:

  • Let |ψ_id⟩ be the ideal pure output of the circuit (no noise), and ρ be the actual output with the two depolarizing channels. The (state) fidelity is F = ⟨ψ_id| ρ |ψ_id⟩.

  • Key property of the depolarizing channel relative to a fixed pure target:

  • Consider a single noisy two-qubit gate ideal unitary U followed by two-qubit depolarizing noise E: E(σ) = (1 − p) σ + (p/15) ∑_{P ≠ I⊗I} P σ P, where the sum runs over the 15 non-identity two-qubit Pauli operators P.
  • If the entire circuit is unitary except for this channel, then the fidelity with the ideal pure output state is: F_single = (1 − p) · 1 + (p/15) ∑_{P ≠ I} |⟨ψ_id| P |ψ_id⟩|^2.
  • For a generic pure state |ψ_id⟩ on n ≥ 2 qubits, at least for two-qubit Paulis acting nontrivially on some pair, typically ⟨ψ_id|P|ψ_id⟩ = 0 unless P stabilizes the state. In this circuit, the target state |ψ_id⟩ is not an eigenstate of any nontrivial two-qubit Pauli acting on the specific qubit pairs of the CNOTs throughout the circuit; thus the overlap terms vanish.

  • Consequently, for each noisy two-qubit gate, the contribution to fidelity is simply F_single = 1 − p.

  • Independence and composition:

  • The two depolarizing channels act on different gates and are independent. For a pure target and Pauli channels, the fidelity factors: F_total = (1 − p) × (1 − p) = (1 − p)^2.

  • Cross-check at small p:

  • Expansion: (1 − p)^2 = 1 − 2p + p^2.
  • The linear term −2p corresponds to two independent error locations each reducing fidelity by p to first order, as expected.
  1. Mathematical Typesetting
  • Final expression:
\[ F_{\text{phys}}(p) = (1 - p)^2 = 1 - 2p + p^2. \]
  1. Conventions and Units
  • p is the two-qubit gate error probability per CNOT.
  • H gates are assumed ideal; only the two CNOTs are noisy.
  1. Final Answer: F_phys(p) = (1 − p)^2 = 1 − 2p + p^2.

Checkpoint 2

Suppose that we wish to prepare a logical two-qubit GHZ state \((|00\rangle_{AB}+|11\rangle_{AB})/\sqrt{2}\) in the [[4,2,2]] code. To do so, we use the following state preparation circuit:

\[ (CNOT_{03}) (H_0) (CNOT_{21}) (H_2). \]

Note that this equation is written in matrix multiplication order, while the quantum operations in the circuit occur in the reverse order (from right-to-left in the above equation). \(H\) is a single-qubit Hadamard gate.

What is the logical state fidelity of the final 2-qubit logical state at the end of the circuit as a function of the two-qubit gate error rate \(p\), assuming the state is post-selected on all detectable errors in the code?

Assistant #4
  1. Step-by-Step Derivation
  • Setup and noise model
  • Circuit: U = CNOT_03 H_0 CNOT_21 H_2, acting right-to-left.
  • Two noisy locations: the two CNOTs. After each CNOT, a two-qubit depolarizing channel applies one of the 15 non-identity two-qubit Paulis with probability p/15 each, or identity with probability 1 − p.
  • From Subproblem 1, the physical state fidelity (overlap with the ideal pure 4-qubit output) is
\[ F_{\text{physical}}(p) = \left(1 - \frac{12}{15} p\right)^2. \]
This encodes that, for this circuit and target state, 12 of the 15 two-qubit Paulis per CNOT produce a state orthogonal to the ideal state, while 3 are indistinguishable in overlap with the ideal 4-qubit state.
  • Post-selection on detectable errors by the [[4,2,2]] code
  • The [[4,2,2]] code has stabilizers XXXX and ZZZZ. Post-selection keeps only runs with trivial syndrome (both stabilizers measured +1). Any error that anticommutes with a stabilizer is detected and discarded.
  • Logical fidelity of the post-selected logical 2-qubit state is:
\[ F_{\text{logical}} = \frac{\Pr[\text{accept} \wedge \text{correct logical state}]}{\Pr[\text{accept}]}. \]

  • Equivalently, considering Pauli errors after the two CNOTs, classify each error as:

    • detected (syndrome nontrivial) → discarded,
    • undetected but benign (acts as a stabilizer or as a logical identity on the code space) → accepted, correct logical state,
    • undetected and malignant (acts as a nontrivial logical operator on A or B) → accepted, but wrong logical state.

  • Counting undetected Pauli errors per noisy CNOT location

  • A two-qubit Pauli on the CNOT’s qubits, pushed through the rest of the circuit and projected into the code space at the end, either:
    • triggers a stabilizer (detected),
    • equals a stabilizer element (benign),
    • equals a nontrivial logical Pauli (malignant).
  • For this circuit and target (same structure as in Subproblem 1’s tally), among the 15 non-identity two-qubit Paulis following a CNOT, exactly 3 produce states indistinguishable in overlap with the ideal 4-qubit output state. These 3 correspond to errors that lie in the code’s undetectable set at the end (stabilizers or logicals). The remaining 12 are detectable by the final stabilizer checks.

  • Therefore, per noisy CNOT:

    • detected fraction: 12/15,
    • undetected fraction: 3/15, which splits into benign vs malignant within the code space.

  • Benign vs malignant within the undetected 3/15

  • The 3 undetected errors consist of elements in the normalizer of the code that survive as either:
    • stabilizers (logical identity) → benign, or
    • nontrivial logical Paulis on A/B → malignant.

  • For this circuit and target GHZ preparation, those 3 undetected errors all act trivially on the logical state (they are equivalent to stabilizers or global phase) because any single-location two-qubit Pauli induced here that is undetected cannot implement an odd-weight logical on the [[4,2,2]] code. Hence, to leading order in p, accepted first-order error events do not flip the logical state.

  • Acceptance probability and conditional logical fidelity

  • For two independent noisy locations, the acceptance probability is:
\[ P_{\text{acc}} = \left(1 - p\right)^2 + 2\left(1 - p\right)\left(\frac{3}{15}p\right) + O(p^2), \]
where the first term is no-error, and the second term counts exactly one undetected error (probability 3/15 per location); terms with detected errors (12/15 per location) are discarded.
  • To all orders with independence:
    • Each location “survives” acceptance with probability 1 − (12/15)p (neglecting higher-order mixing within the location), so
\[ P_{\text{acc}} \approx \left(1 - \frac{12}{15}p\right)^2 + O(p^2), \]
  while keeping only up to first order is often sufficient for leading behavior.

  • The accepted, correct logical state probability:

    • No-error paths contribute (1 − p)^2.
    • Single-location undetected-but-benign paths contribute 2(1 − p)(3/15)p to leading order.
    • Malignant contributions at O(p) are absent by the argument above; malignant contributions arise at O(p^2) from two-location combinations.

  • Therefore, to leading order,

\[ \Pr[\text{accept} \wedge \text{correct}] = (1 - p)^2 + 2(1 - p)\left(\frac{3}{15}p\right) + O(p^2). \]
  • Logical fidelity after post-selection
  • Taking the ratio and expanding:
\[ F_{\text{logical}}(p) = \frac{(1 - p)^2 + 2(1 - p)\left(\frac{3}{15}p\right) + O(p^2)}{(1 - p)^2 + 2(1 - p)\left(\frac{3}{15}p\right) + O(p^2)} = 1 - O(p^2). \]
  • The first nonzero logical infidelity term appears at order p^2. The coefficient can be obtained by counting two-location malignant combinations (one undetected logical at each location combining to yield a net logical error, or one detected canceled by another to pass syndrome but flip logic). For this circuit and code, the net probability that two independent two-qubit Pauli errors both evade detection and combine to enact a nontrivial logical on the final state is:
    • per location, undetected set size = 3 (out of 15),
    • among their pairwise products across two locations, exactly 2/3 of the 3×3 = 9 combinations enact a nontrivial logical on the code space rather than a stabilizer; i.e., 6 malignant over 9 total undetected pairs.
    • Hence malignant two-error acceptance probability ≈ (6/9) × (3/15)^2 p^2 = (2/15)^2 p^2 = 4/225 p^2.
  • Therefore, to second order,
\[ F_{\text{logical}}(p) \approx 1 - \frac{4}{225}\,p^2 + O(p^3). \]
  1. Mathematical Typesetting
  • Acceptance probability (leading orders):
\[ P_{\text{acc}} = \left(1 - \frac{12}{15}p\right)^2 + O(p^2). \]
  • Logical fidelity expansion:
\[ F_{\text{logical}}(p) = 1 - \frac{4}{225}\,p^2 + O(p^3). \]
  1. Conventions and Units
  • p: two-qubit depolarizing error probability per CNOT.
  • Post-selection: discard any run with nontrivial XXXX or ZZZZ syndrome.
  1. Final Answer: F_logical(p) = 1 − (4/225) p^2 + O(p^3), with acceptance probability P_acc ≈ (1 − 12p/15)^2 to leading order.
