Problem setup:

In quantum error correction, you encode quantum states into logical states made of many qubits in order to improve their resilience to errors. In quantum error detection, you do the same but can only detect the presence of errors and not correct them. In this problem, we will consider a single [[4,2,2]] quantum error detection code, which encodes two logical qubits into four physical qubits, and investigate how robust logical quantum operations in this code are to quantum errors.

Our convention is that the four physical qubits in the [[4,2,2]] code are labelled 0,1,2,3. The two logical qubits are labelled A and B. The stabilizers are \(XXXX\) and \(ZZZZ\), where \(X\) and \(Z\) are Pauli matrices. The logical \(X\) and \(Z\) operators on the two qubits are \(X_A = XIXI\), \(X_B=XXII\), \(Z_A = ZZII\), \(Z_B = ZIZI\), up to multiplication by stabilizers.

We will consider different state preparation circuits consisting of controlled not \(CNOT_{ij}\) gates, where \(CNOT_{ij}\) has control qubit \(i\) and target qubit \(j\). As a simple model of quantum errors in hardware, we will suppose that each \(CNOT_{ij}\) gate in the circuit has a two qubit depolarizing error channel following it that produces one of the 15 non-identity two-qubit Paulis with equal probability \(p/15\). The probability \(p\) indicates the probability of an error in a single two-qubit gate. We will assess the logical infidelity of certain state preparation protocols as a function of the physical infidelity \(p\).

Checkpoint 1

1. Noiseless circuit and ideal output state
We start from the physical input

\[ |\psi_{\rm in}\rangle = |0_0\,0_1\,0_2\,0_3\rangle \]

and apply, in order (right–to–left),

\[ U = \bigl(\mathrm{CNOT}_{0\to3}\bigr)\,(H_0)\,\bigl(\mathrm{CNOT}_{2\to1}\bigr)\,(H_2)\,. \]

Labeling qubits as \((0,1,2,3)\):

  1. After \(H_2\) on qubit 2:
\[ |\psi_1\rangle = \frac1{\sqrt2}\bigl|0\,0\,0\,0\bigr\rangle + \frac1{\sqrt2}\bigl|0\,0\,1\,0\bigr\rangle. \]
  1. After \(\mathrm{CNOT}_{2\to1}\) (control\,=\,2, target\,=\,1):
\[ |\psi_2\rangle = \frac1{\sqrt2}\bigl|0\,0\,0\,0\bigr\rangle + \frac1{\sqrt2}\bigl|0\,1\,1\,0\bigr\rangle. \]
  1. After \(H_0\) on qubit 0:
\[ |\psi_3\rangle = \frac12\Bigl( |0\,0\,0\,0\rangle + |1\,0\,0\,0\rangle +|0\,1\,1\,0\rangle + |1\,1\,1\,0\rangle \Bigr). \]
  1. After \(\mathrm{CNOT}_{0\to3}\) (control = 0, target = 3):
\[ |\psi\rangle = \frac12\Bigl( |0\,0\,0\,0\rangle + |1\,0\,0\,1\rangle +|0\,1\,1\,0\rangle + |1\,1\,1\,1\rangle \Bigr). \]

One checks immediately that

\[ |\psi\rangle = \frac{|0_0\,0_3\rangle + |1_0\,1_3\rangle}{\sqrt2}\;\otimes\; \frac{|0_1\,0_2\rangle + |1_1\,1_2\rangle}{\sqrt2} = |\Phi^+\rangle_{0,3}\;\otimes\;|\Phi^+\rangle_{1,2}\,, \]

i.e.\ the final state is a product of two Bell pairs on qubit–pairs \((0,3)\) and \((1,2)\).

2. Noise model
Each CNOT gate is followed by an independent two‐qubit depolarizing channel on its control–target pair. Concretely, if \(G\) is a noiseless CNOT on qubits \((i,j)\) then the noisy gate acts as

\[ \mathcal{E}_{ij}(\rho) = (1-p)\,G\,\rho\,G^\dagger + \frac{p}{15}\sum_{(a,b)\neq(0,0)} \bigl(\sigma_a^{(i)}\otimes\sigma_b^{(j)}\bigr)\, G\,\rho\,G^\dagger\, \bigl(\sigma_a^{(i)}\otimes\sigma_b^{(j)}\bigr)\!, \]

where \(\{\sigma_0,\sigma_1,\sigma_2,\sigma_3\}=\{I,X,Y,Z\}\) and \(p\) is the two‐qubit error rate.

Since the two CNOTs act on disjoint qubit‐pairs, and the Hadamards are assumed perfect, all errors on \((2,1)\) commute through the remaining gates to the end as Paulis on qubits \((1,2)\), and likewise errors on \((0,3)\) remain on that pair. Thus the final density matrix is

\[ \rho_{\rm out} =\sum_{P\in\{I,X,Y,Z\}^{\otimes2}}\sum_{Q\in\{I,X,Y,Z\}^{\otimes2}} \;w_{P}\,w_{Q}\; \bigl(Q_{0,3}\otimes P_{1,2}\bigr)\, |\psi\rangle\langle\psi|\, \bigl(Q_{0,3}\otimes P_{1,2}\bigr), \]

where

\[ w_{I\,I} = 1-p,\quad w_{P\neq I\,I} = \frac p{15} \]

for each two‐qubit Pauli \(P\) (resp.\ \(Q\)).

3. Fidelity as a sum over Pauli‐error patterns
The physical fidelity is

\[ F(p) = \langle\psi|\rho_{\rm out}|\psi\rangle = \sum_{P,Q} w_{P}\,w_{Q}\; \bigl|\langle\psi|(Q_{0,3}\otimes P_{1,2})|\psi\rangle\bigr|^2. \]

But \(|\psi\rangle = |\Phi^+\rangle_{0,3}\otimes|\Phi^+\rangle_{1,2}\), and on each Bell pair the only Pauli operators that satisfy

\[ \sigma\otimes\sigma\;|\Phi^+\rangle = \pm|\Phi^+\rangle \]

are precisely \(\{\;I\otimes I,\;X\otimes X,\;Y\otimes Y,\;Z\otimes Z\}\). Hence:

– No errors (\(P=I\otimes I\), \(Q=I\otimes I\)):
weight \((1-p)^2\), contributes \((1-p)^2\).

– Single error on \((1,2)\) only (\(P\neq I\!I\), \(Q=I\!I\)):
there are 15 non‐identity \(P\), of which 3 (\(XX,YY,ZZ\)) lie in the Bell–pair stabilizer.
total contribution

\[ (1-p)\;\frac p{15}\;\times 3 = \frac p5\,(1-p). \]

– Single error on \((0,3)\) only (\(P=I\!I\), \(Q\neq I\!I\)):
likewise contributes

\[ \frac p5\,(1-p). \]

– Errors on both pairs (\(P\neq I\!I\), \(Q\neq I\!I\)):
there are \(3\times3=9\) favorable choices among the \(15\times15=225\) total, each with weight \((p/15)^2\).
total contribution

\[ 9\;\bigl(\tfrac p{15}\bigr)^2 = \frac{p^2}{25}. \]

Summing all contributions:

\[ F(p) = (1-p)^2 + 2\,\frac p5\,(1-p) + \frac{p^2}{25} = 1 - \frac{8}{5}\,p + \frac{16}{25}\,p^2. \]

Final Answer:

\[ \boxed{% F(p)\;=\;(1-p)^2 \;+\;\frac{2p}{5}\,(1-p)\;+\;\frac{p^2}{25} \;=\;1 \;-\;\tfrac{8}{5}\,p \;+\;\tfrac{16}{25}\,p^2 }\,. \]

Step-by-Step Derivation

We keep the same notation as in Subproblem 1. After the two CNOTs (each followed by its depolarizing‐error channel) and the two perfect Hadamards, the net error on the four physical qubits factors as

\[ E \;=\;E_{0,3}\;E_{1,2} \,,\qquad E_{1,2}\;\in\;\{I,X,Y,Z\}_{1}\otimes\{I,X,Y,Z\}_{2}, \quad E_{0,3}\;\in\;\{I,X,Y,Z\}_{0}\otimes\{I,X,Y,Z\}_{3}, \]

with

\[ \Pr\bigl[E_{i,j}=I\otimes I\bigr]=1-p, \quad \Pr\bigl[E_{i,j}=P\neq I\otimes I\bigr]=\frac p{15}\,. \]

We then post‐select on the code‐space by measuring the stabilizers

\[ S_X=X_0X_1X_2X_3,\quad S_Z=Z_0Z_1Z_2Z_3 \]

and keeping only those runs where both outcomes are \(+1\). Equivalently we retain exactly those Pauli errors \(E\) which commute with both \(S_X\) and \(S_Z\).

  1. Commutation constraints

A Pauli \(E\) of the form \(E_{0,3}\,E_{1,2}\) commutes with
\(\,S_X\) iff the total number of single‐qubit anticommutations with \(X\) is even,
and similarly commutes with \(S_Z\) iff the total number of anticommutations with \(Z\) is even.
Equivalently one checks that

\[ E_{0,3}\,E_{1,2}\;\text{accepted} \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \begin{cases} \alpha_{0,3}+\alpha_{1,2}\equiv0\pmod2,\\ \beta_{0,3}+\beta_{1,2}\equiv0\pmod2, \end{cases} \]

where for any two‐qubit Pauli \(P\) on \(\{i,j\}\) we define

\[ \alpha(P)= \bigl[\text{\# of \(Z\)–or–\(Y\) on }i,j\bigr]\!\bmod2,\quad \beta(P)= \bigl[\text{\# of \(X\)–or–\(Y\) on }i,j\bigr]\!\bmod2. \]

Thus we group the 16 Pauli‐pairs on qubits \((1,2)\) by their \((\alpha,\beta)\in\{0,1\}^2\), and likewise on \((0,3)\).

  1. Marginal weights

Denote

\[ S_{p,q} =\sum_{\substack{P\in\{I,X,Y,Z\}^{\otimes2}\\\alpha(P)=p,\;\beta(P)=q}} \Pr[P] \quad(p,q\in\{0,1\}). \]

A direct enumeration shows

\[ S_{0,0}=(1-p)+3\frac p{15}=1-\frac{4p}5, \qquad S_{0,1}=S_{1,0}=S_{1,1} =4\;\frac p{15}=\frac{4p}{15}, \]

and indeed \(\sum_{p,q}S_{p,q}=1\).

  1. Acceptance probability

Since the two error‐pairs are independent, the probability that \(\alpha_{0,3}=\alpha_{1,2}\) and \(\beta_{0,3}=\beta_{1,2}\) is

\[ p_{\rm acc} =\sum_{p,q\in\{0,1\}}S_{p,q}^2 =\Bigl(1-\tfrac{4p}5\Bigr)^2 \;+\;3\Bigl(\tfrac{4p}{15}\Bigr)^2 =1-\frac{8p}5+\frac{64p^2}{75}. \]
  1. Probability of a trivial (stabilizer) error

The only accepted errors which act trivially on the logical subspace are the four elements of the stabilizer group, \(\{I,\;X_0X_1X_2X_3,\;Z_0Z_1Z_2Z_3,\;Y_0Y_1Y_2Y_3\}\). These arise exactly when

\[ (E_{1,2},E_{0,3}) \;=\; (I\!I,I\!I),\;(X\,X,X\,X),\;(Z\,Z,Z\,Z),\;(Y\,Y,Y\,Y), \]

each such pattern having probability \((1-p)^2\) for \((I\!I,I\!I)\) and \(\bigl(\tfrac p{15}\bigr)^2\) for each of the other three. Hence

\[ p_{\rm triv} =(1-p)^2+3\Bigl(\tfrac p{15}\Bigr)^2 =(1-p)^2+\frac{p^2}{75} =1-2p+\frac{76}{75}p^2. \]
  1. Logical fidelity under post‐selection

Conditioned on acceptance, the fidelity of the logical two‐qubit state with the ideal GHZ is the probability that no nontrivial logical operator has acted. Hence

\[ F_{\rm logical}(p) =\frac{p_{\rm triv}}{p_{\rm acc}} =\frac{(1-p)^2+\tfrac{p^2}{75}} {1-\tfrac{8p}5+\tfrac{64p^2}{75}} =\frac{1-2p+\tfrac{76}{75}p^2} {1-\tfrac{8}{5}p+\tfrac{64}{75}p^2}. \]

Final Answer:

\[ \boxed{ F_{\rm logical}(p) =\frac{(1-p)^2+\dfrac{p^2}{75}} {1-\dfrac{8}{5}p+\dfrac{64}{75}p^2} \;=\; \frac{1-2p+\tfrac{76}{75}p^2} {1-\tfrac{8}{5}p+\tfrac{64}{75}p^2}. \]

What is the logical state fidelity of the final 2-qubit logical state at the end of the circuit as a function of two-qubit gate error rate \(p\), assuming the state is post-selected on all detectable errors in the code and on the ancilla qubit measuring \(|0\rangle\)?

Step‐by‐Step Derivation

We label the five CNOTs (in execution order) as

1. G₁ ≡ CNOT₁→₂
2. G₂ ≡ CNOT₁→₀
3. G₃ ≡ CNOT₂→₃
4. G₄ ≡ CNOT₃→₄
5. G₅ ≡ CNOT₀→₄

Each CNOT \(G_k\) is followed by an independent two‐qubit depolarizing error on its two qubits, which applies one of the 15 non‐identity Paulis uniformly with probability \(p/15\), or does nothing with probability \(1-p\). The Hadamard and the final ancilla measurement are taken to be perfect.

We post‐select on (a) the ancilla‐qubit \(4\) measuring \(|0\>_4\), and (b) the two code‐stabilizers

\[ S_X = X_0X_1X_2X_3,\qquad S_Z = Z_0Z_1Z_2Z_3 \]

both returning the “\(+1\)” outcome. Equivalently we reject any run in which a single‐fault anywhere in the gadget flips the ancilla or yields a nontrivial syndrome on \(\{0,1,2,3\}\).

1. No accepted single errors.
By construction the circuit is fault‐tolerant in detection mode: any single two‐qubit error on one of the five CNOTs either (i) propagates an \(X\)–type fault onto the ancilla (and so flips its \(Z\)–measurement), or (ii) leaves a single‐qubit Pauli on \(\{0,1,2,3\}\) which anticommutes with either \(S_X\) or \(S_Z\). Hence all single‐fault events are caught and rejected.

2. The first possible undetected logical faults occur at weight‐2.
We must look at pairs of CNOTs whose simultaneous two‐qubit errors can (i) cancel on the ancilla so the ancilla still reads \(|0\>\), and (ii) combine into a two‐qubit Pauli on \(\{0,1,2,3\}\) of the form \(P\otimes P\) (\(P\in\{X,Y,Z\}\)) on two data‐qubits, which commutes with both \(S_X\) and \(S_Z\) and so slips the stabilizer check. Such a \(P\otimes P\) on two data‐qubits is exactly a logical operator (up to stabilizers) and so causes a logical error.

A short “by‐inspection” on the five‐gate gadget shows that the only gate‐pairs whose faults can both evade ancilla‐ and code‐detection are the three data–only pairs

\[ (G_1,G_2),\;(G_1,G_3),\;(G_2,G_3), \]

i.e.\ the three CNOTs among qubits \(\{0,1,2,3\}\) before the ancilla couplings. On each of those three pairs one checks:

– There are exactly six ways to choose a non‐identity Pauli on each of the two CNOTs so that, after propagation through the later gates, the net error on \(\{0,1,2,3\}\) is one of

\[ X_iX_j,\;Y_iY_j,\;Z_iZ_j \]
  on some pair \(\{i,j\}\subset\{0,1,2,3\}\).  In each of these six ways the two ancilla‐CNOTs induce no net flip on qubit 4, and the two‐qubit Pauli on the data commutes with both \(S_X\) and \(S_Z\).  Moreover each such \(P\otimes P\) is a **logical** operator (not a stabilizer) on the [[4,2,2]] code.

– All other double‐fault combinations either (a) flip the ancilla, or (b) produce a Pauli on the data which anticommutes with one of the stabilizers, and so are rejected.

Hence each of the three data–only gate‐pairs contributes exactly \(6\) undetected, logical‐fault events.

3. Weight‐2 probabilities.
– Probability no gate fails: \((1-p)^5\).
– Probability exactly two gates fail at positions \((k,\ell)\) with specific non-identity Pauli choices on each is

\[ (1-p)^3\;\bigl(\tfrac p{15}\bigr)^2. \]

– Out of the \(\binom{5}{2}=10\) gate‐pairs only 3 pairs are dangerous, and each of those contributes exactly \(6\) ways. Hence the total probability of an undetected logical fault at weight 2 is

\[ P_{\rm err,acc} \;=\; 3\times 6\;\bigl(\tfrac p{15}\bigr)^2 \;=\;\frac{18}{225}\,p^2 \;=\;\frac{2}{25}\,p^2\;+\;O(p^3). \]

Meanwhile the probability to accept (zero or undetected faults) is

\[ P_{\rm acc} \;=\; (1-p)^5 \;+\; \underbrace{3\times 6}_{=18}\;\bigl(\tfrac p{15}\bigr)^2 \;+\;O(p^3) \;=\;(1-p)^5+\frac{2}{25}\,p^2 \;+\;O(p^3). \]

4. Logical fidelity under post-selection.
By definition

\[ F_{\rm logical}(p) \;=\; \frac{P_{\rm acc}-P_{\rm err,acc}}{P_{\rm acc}} \;=\; 1 \;-\; \frac{P_{\rm err,acc}}{P_{\rm acc}} \;=\; 1\;-\; \frac{\displaystyle\frac{2}{25}\,p^2 + O(p^3)} {\;(1-p)^5 + \frac{2}{25}\,p^2 + O(p^3)\!}\,. \]

Expanding to leading non‐trivial order in \(p\),

\[ F_{\rm logical}(p) = 1 \;-\; \frac{2}{25}\,p^2 + O(p^3). \]

Final Answer:

To second order in the physical error rate \(p\), the post-selected logical fidelity is

\[ \boxed{ F_{\rm logical}(p)\;=\;1 \;-\;\frac{2}{25}\,p^2 \;+\;O(p^3). } \]
