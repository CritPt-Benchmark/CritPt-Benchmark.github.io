Problem setup:

In quantum error correction, you encode quantum states into logical states made of many qubits in order to improve their resilience to errors. In quantum error detection, you do the same but can only detect the presence of errors and not correct them. In this problem, we will consider a single [[4,2,2]] quantum error detection code, which encodes two logical qubits into four physical qubits, and investigate how robust logical quantum operations in this code are to quantum errors.

Our convention is that the four physical qubits in the [[4,2,2]] code are labelled 0,1,2,3. The two logical qubits are labelled A and B. The stabilizers are \(XXXX\) and \(ZZZZ\), where \(X\) and \(Z\) are Pauli matrices. The logical \(X\) and \(Z\) operators on the two qubits are \(X_A = XIXI\), \(X_B=XXII\), \(Z_A = ZZII\), \(Z_B = ZIZI\), up to multiplication by stabilizers.

We will consider different state preparation circuits consisting of controlled not \(CNOT_{ij}\) gates, where \(CNOT_{ij}\) has control qubit \(i\) and target qubit \(j\). As a simple model of quantum errors in hardware, we will suppose that each \(CNOT_{ij}\) gate in the circuit has a two qubit depolarizing error channel following it that produces one of the 15 non-identity two-qubit Paulis with equal probability \(p/15\). The probability \(p\) indicates the probability of an error in a single two-qubit gate. We will assess the logical infidelity of certain state preparation protocols as a function of the physical infidelity \(p\).

Checkpoint 1

Suppose that we wish to prepare a logical two-qubit GHZ state \((|00\rangle_{AB}+|11\rangle_{AB})/\sqrt{2}\) in the [[4,2,2]] code. To do so, we use the following state preparation circuit:

\[ (CNOT_{03}) (H_0) (CNOT_{21}) (H_2). \]

Note that this equation is written in matrix multiplication order, while the quantum operations in the circuit occur in the reverse order (from right-to-left in the above equation). \(H\) is a single-qubit Hadamard gate.

What is the physical state fidelity of the final physical 4-qubit state at the end of the circuit as a function of the two-qubit gate error rate \(p\)?

1. The ideal prepared state

Starting from the vacuum \(|0000\rangle\), the perfect circuit

\[ U \;=\;CNOT_{03}\;H_0\;CNOT_{21}\;H_2 \]

produces a [[4,2,2]] code word that encodes the logical twoâ€“qubit GHZ state

\[ |\psi_{\text{ideal}}\rangle_{AB} \;=\;\frac{|00\rangle_{AB}+|11\rangle_{AB}}{\sqrt2}\;. \]

The physical fourâ€“qubit state will be denoted \(|\psi\rangle\equiv|\psi_{\text{ideal}}\rangle\).

2. Error model and fidelity formula

â€¢ Every CNOT is followed by an independent twoâ€“qubit depolarizing channel

\[ \mathcal E(\rho)\;=\;(1-p)\,\rho +\frac{p}{15}\sum_{P\neq I\!\!I}P\,\rho\,P , \]

where the sum runs over the \(15\) non-identity twoâ€“qubit Paulis acting on the same pair of qubits as that CNOT.

â€¢ There are only two noisy gates, hence two independent error operators
\(E_{21}\) (acting on qubits \(2,1\)) and \(E_{03}\) (acting on \(0,3\)) with

\[ \Pr[E_{ij}=I\!\!I]=(1-p),\qquad \Pr[E_{ij}=P\neq I\!\!I]=\frac{p}{15}. \]

Because qubits \(\{2,1\}\) and \(\{0,3\}\) are disjoint and all gates are Clifford, both error operators can be moved to the end of the circuit without changing their form. The final state therefore is

\[ \rho_{\text{out}} =\sum_{E_{21},E_{03}}\Pr(E_{21})\Pr(E_{03}) \;E_{03}E_{21}\,|\psi\rangle\!\langle\psi|\,E_{21}E_{03}. \]

For a pure target state the (state) fidelity is

\[ F(p)=\langle\psi|\rho_{\text{out}}|\psi\rangle =\sum_{E_{21},E_{03}}\Pr(E_{21})\Pr(E_{03}) \,\,f(E_{21}E_{03}), \qquad f(P)\equiv|\langle\psi|P|\psi\rangle|^{2}. \]

3. Which Paulis leave \(|\psi\rangle\) unchanged?

The code is stabilised by

\[ S=\langle XXXX,\;ZZZZ\rangle , \]

and the logical operators are (up to stabilisers)

\[ X_A=X I X I,\; X_B=XX I I,\qquad Z_A=ZZ I I,\; Z_B=Z I Z I . \]

For the GHZ logical state one has

\[ X_A X_B|\psi\rangle=|\psi\rangle,\quad Z_A Z_B|\psi\rangle=|\psi\rangle,\quad Y_A Y_B|\psi\rangle=-|\psi\rangle . \]

Multiplying these by stabilisers shows that the six two-qubit Paulis

\[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal C_{12}&=\{\,I X X I,\;I Y Y I,\;I Z Z I\},\\ \mathcal C_{03}&=\{\,X I I X,\;Y I I Y,\;Z I I Z\}, \end{aligned} \]

as well as the three weight-4 stabilisers
\(XXXX,\;YYYY,\;ZZZZ\), act as \(\pm1\) on \(|\psi\rangle\).
Therefore

\[ f(P)=1\quad\text{iff}\quad P\in \{I\}\cup\mathcal C_{12}\cup\mathcal C_{03}\cup \{XXXX,YYYY,ZZZZ\}, \]

and \(f(P)=0\) otherwise.

4. Counting the contributing error configurations

Write \(C\) for the set \(\mathcal C_{12}\) or \(\mathcal C_{03}\) and \(N\) for the remaining \(12\) non-identity two-qubit Paulis on a given pair.

  1. No error
    â€¢ probability \((1-p)^2\),
    â€¢ contribution to \(F\): \(1\).

  2. Exactly one (benign) error
    â€¢ probability \(2(1-p)\dfrac{p}{15}\times|C|=2(1-p)p\dfrac{3}{15}\),
    â€¢ contribution: \(1\).

  3. Two errors

There are \(15^{2}=225\) ordered pairs \((E_{21},E_{03})\) with both errors non-identity. A direct parity count (or an explicit table) shows that
\(57\) of these pairs multiply to an operator with \(f=1\) (all others give \(f=0\)):
â€¢ \((C,C)\) with identical letters \(:\) \(3\times3=9\),
â€¢ \((N,N)\) pairs whose parities match the stabiliser conditions \(:\) \(48\).
The probability of such double-error events is \(\Bigl(\dfrac{p}{15}\Bigr)^{2}\) for each pair.

Putting everything together,

\[ \begin{aligned} F(p) &=(1-p)^2 +2(1-p)p\frac{3}{15} +\frac{57}{225}\,p^{2}\\[2mm] &=1-\frac{8}{5}\,p+\frac{64}{75}\,p^{2}. \end{aligned} \]

5. Result

The physical fourâ€“qubit state fidelity after the preparation circuit, as a function of the two-qubit gate error probability \(p\), is

\[ \boxed{\,F(p)=1-\frac{8}{5}\,p+\frac{64}{75}\,p^{2}\,}. \]

This expression is exact, since at most two faulty CNOTs occur in the circuit.

Final Answer:
The physical state fidelity is
\(F(p)=1-\dfrac{8}{5}\,p+\dfrac{64}{75}\,p^{2}\;\).
